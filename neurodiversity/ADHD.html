ADHD (Attention Defecit Hyperactivity Disorder)

By Cie Buckland

What is it?

ADHD is not a disease! ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) is a neurodevelopmental neurotype that is recognised by a person’s lack of neurotransmitters in their brain, as well as a natural brain chemical called dopamine.

The result of these unique characterisitcs of the brain, is shown through ADHDers usually having the inability to regulate their attention span, as well as working memory, motivation and interest. Having a natural lack of dopamine, a brain chemical and neurotransmitter (as well as some other things going on in the prefrontal cortex), can lead to the unbalanced completion of activities and cognitive processes. This might look like starting a problem with great interest and high motivation, and then losing all intertest and motivation half-way through. Or maybe not being able to focus in class, but at home, focus on the home work or on a personal project for hours at a time, without getting distracted once.

This behaviour of focusing for hours on end and not giving in to distraction, is called hyperfocus, and it's apart of the difficulties of regulating an attention span. But it sure comes in handy for cramming before a test! Not that I recommend it.

So, it's basically all or nothing; hyperfocus or zero focus. ADHDers can also sometimes experience impulsive decision-making or actions, whether that be impulse purchasing, impuslive decisions in a tricky situation or fight, etc. ADHD can occur in children (both boys and girls), as well as adults.

Different Types of ADHD

Experiences can vary for any ADHDer, especially since there are several types of ADHD. There are two distinct types of ADHD (hyperactive or inattentive), but they can overlap in some areas, and when they overlap significantly, we get something called "combined ADHD". So, to give you a bit more info, here are the three types of ADHD, and what they usually entail:

  • Number #1 - Hyperactive ADHD, is the least-common type, and it's where the person tends to be more physically hyperactive. And this can be seen through fidgeting, non-stop energy and impulsive and hyperactive behaviours.

  • Number #2 - Inattentive ADHD (or ADD), where the person has a shorter or selective memory, polar attention span (they either hyper-focus or find it almost impossible to focus), and have what I like to call a "racing-mind", where their thoughts are constantly racing around, thinking of like a hundred different things at once.
    This usually makes it hard to finish a train of thought, and it can result in being very talkative as they try to get all their ideas and thoughts out at once, or being very quiet as they are so immersed in their busy mind.

  • Number #3 - Combined ADHD, or co-occurring ADHD, is the most common type of ADHD, and it's where the two types of inattentive and hyperactive overlap significantly, and result in the ADHDer experiencing traits that are found on both hyperactive ADHD and inattentive ADHD.
    For example, someone with combined ADHD might be hyperactive with fidgeting, and non-stop energy, but might also have a really short attention span as they try to focus, but their brain just wants to turn to something more stimulating whether it be a physical activty or a topic that generates more interest.

Common ADHD Types in Boys and Girls

Boys and girls can both have ADHD, but in most cases, they is a certain type of ADHD that is shown more in one gender, than the other.
Hyperactive ADHD and Combined ADHD, are 2 to 3 times more common in boys. Whereas in girls, Inattentive ADHD is more common (but this does not mean that a girl can’t have hyperactive ADHD and a boy can't have inattentive ADHD; every brain is different).

Unfortunately, this has developed a misguided idea that boys are more likely to have ADHD since the common ADHD type (hyperactive) in boys is presented more physically and therefore easier to pickup and diagnose, while the Inattentive type which is common in girls, has more internalised symptoms. This then leads to a misdiagnosis and underdiagnosis in girls with ADHD, which has lead to the average age of seven for boys to with ADHD to be diagnosed, while most girls and women struggle with a lack of support and diagnosis, or being misdiagnosed, until they, on average receive a correct diagnois for ADHD in their thirties or fourties.

But this can change, by raising awareness about ADHD and educating people on it and how it presents differently, ADHD in girls can be recognised and diagnosed a lot sooner, resulting in a life where they have the support they need and are given the chances they deserve to excel in areas of life and school just like their peers.

How does the ADHD brain work?

The prefrontal cortex plays a particularly significant role in controlling cognitive abilities such as working memory, emotional control, and complex problem-solving. The abilities we all rely on to manage daily chores, such as time management, staying organised, and planning, are known as executive functions.

In children who are ADHDers, the prefrontal cortex develops more slowly and is slightly smaller in size. Additionally, children who are ADHDers have reduced volumes of the cerebellum, hippocampus, and amygdala.

What is the Prefrontal Cortex responsible for?

The Prefrontal Cortex is responsible for:

  • Regulating our thoughts, actions and emotions through extensive connections to other brain regions
  • Decision making
  • Working memory
  • Personality expression and moderating social behaviour
  • Controlling certain aspects of speech and language
  • Attention span/the focus of one’s attention
  • Predicting the consequences of an action and anticipating events in an environment
  • Impulse control
  • Managing emotional reactions
  • Planning for the future

Debunking the “dumb” myth

Over the years, as doctors and scientists have learned more about ADHD and what it entails, people and society have gained a better understanding of what it actually is. Unfortunately, there are still some ideas that ADHD is just an “excuse” to get out of doing things, to be lazy, or that people with ADHD are less intelligent and unable to learn.
However, all of those ideas are completely false. There is scientific evidence and research supporting the reasons behind the lack of attention and retention of information.

Just because ADHDers have difficulty paying attention and/or retaining things doesn’t mean that they can’t learn or are limited in their learning. In fact, being an ADHDer, only opens up more doors to explore.
ADHDers tend to process information slower than other people in order to understand it at the level that is needed. And while this can sometimes be frustrating and take time, it means that when going through something slowly, ADHDers can pick up on details that are usually overlooked by others, make connections that are usually missed, and gain a deep understanding of what task or topic they are completing/studying. So even though a work project might be late, it's quality will be above expectations.
