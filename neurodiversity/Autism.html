Autism (ASD - Autsim Spectrum Disorder)

By Cie Buckland

What is it?

Autism, or ASD (the new name), is not a disease or a mental illness; however, some Autistic people could have additional mental health problems. Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a developmental ability, brought on by differences in the brain. Simply put, it means that an Autistic person experiences the world differently, both in how they see it and how they interact with it. Autism impacts a person's thinking, feelings, social interactions, and perception of their surroundings. It is a neurotype that a person has for the whole of their life, from birth until old age.

Some people with Autism have a recognised distinction, such as a genetic condition. Other factors are still unknown. Autism is thought to have a number of underlying reasons that, when combined, alter how people typically develop. There is still so much to learn about these factors and how they affect Autistic people.

The Autism Spectrum

Each Autistic individual is unique. Because of this, autism is referred to as a "spectrum". There are many different characteristics, so it can't be defined in a box. ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder) is the new term for all the different levels or characteristics on the autism “spectrum” (however, some people prefer the name 'Autism'), including what was formerly considered to be a separate diagnosis; Asperger’s Syndrome. Each person with Autism has a unique constellation of these characteristics.

How does an Autistic brain work?

In Autistic people, there are more variations in the brain. So let’s just do a quick biology crash course: There are four lobes—frontal, parietal, occipital, and temporal—within each half. Structures that control everything from movement to thought are located inside these lobes. The cerebral cortex, often known as grey matter, is located above the lobes. Information processing takes place in this area. The cerebral cortex's surface area is increased by the folds in the brain. More information can be processed the larger the surface area, or grey matter.

Going into the more technical side of things, grey matter can form peaks and troughs, which are called gyri and sulci. These creases and wrinkles might take diverse forms in Autistic people, according to San Diego State University researchers. Particularly, there is much greater folding in the right frontal and temporal areas of autistic brains, as well as in the left parietal and temporal lobes.

Because there are differences in the cerebral Cortex/the grey matter, in the brains of Autistic people, the way they process information, view the world, and interact with it will be different too.

What is the Cerebral Cortex responsible for?

The Cerebral Cortex is responsible for:

  • Determining intelligence and personality
  • Motor function
  • Planning and organisation
  • Touch sensation
  • Processing sensory information
  • Language processing
  • Memory
  • Reasoning
  • Decision making
  • Emotion

Debunking the “dumb” myth

The myth that Autistic people are unable to learn is just a plain lie. Unfortunately, some people still believe that having Autism prevents their ability to learn, grow, and discover new talents and skills. But remember, just because they learn differently or experience things differently, doesn’t mean that they can’t learn anything. If we all learned and experienced things the same way, then there wouldn’t be anything new to learn at all.

Please learn from this or keep in mind that everyone's ability to learn is unique and that autism is a spectrum, it's not set to specific abilities. Understanding a person's requirements, skills, and learning style is essential while educating someone, especially someone who's neurodivergent, as we might need some more sepcific support needs, in order to have a fair chance to do our best. To reach the same degree of learning, people with Autism may need additional specific support needs, although some people might not. Some could be even easier to teach than allistic people. Autistic individuals who struggle with learning can also benefit from and advance through effective and qualified supporting courses, set at their own pace. Strengths:

  • Pattern recognition
  • Lateral thinking
  • Very observational
  • Deeply focused
  • Good at absorbing and retaining information

  • Satoshi Tajiri
