Autism (ASD - Autism Spectrum Disorder)

By Cie Buckland

What is it?

Autism, or ASD (the new name), is not a disease or a mental illness; however, some Autistic people could have additional mental health problems. Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a life-long developmental ability, brought on by differences in the brain. Simply put, it means that an Autistic person experiences the world differently, both in how they see it and how they interact with it. Autism impacts a person's thinking, feelings, social interactions, and perception of their surroundings.

Some people with Autism have a recognised distinction, such as a genetic condition. Other factors are still unknown. Autism is thought to have a number of underlying reasons that, when combined, alter how people typically develop. There is still so much to learn about these factors and how they affect Autistic people.

The Autism Spectrum

Each Autistic individual is unique. Because of this, autism is referred to as a "spectrum". There are many different characteristics, and each person has a constellation of them, so it can't be defined in a box. ASD is the new term for all the different levels or characteristics on the autism “spectrum” (however, some people prefer the name 'Autism'), including what was formerly considered to be a separate diagnosis; Asperger’s Syndrome.

Autism in Girls and Boys

Similar to ADHD, symptoms for Autism present differently in girls than they do in boys, and it can be hard to pick them out as they tend to be more internalised, and girls pick up masking and compensation habits much quicker and more intensely than boys, as they feel the need to imitate atypical social behaviours in order to fit in and be accepted.

Autistic symptoms in girls are hard to pick out because they also tend to be socially acceptable, their behaviour might be shy, quiet or withdrawn, and wanting to keep their hands clean in a messy classroom activity; things that are seen as "typical" behaviours for girls. Their passionate interests might be on things that girls "typically" enjoy, like unicorns or princesses, but the difference is how they go about their interest compared to atypical girls.

Combine the masking habits with the internalised symptoms and signs that seem to fit within society's expectations, and it makes it hard to notice and diagnose Autism in girls. It can lead to an undiagnosis or misdiagnosis in girls, which is also pushed by the fact that there is also a gender bias in the research facilities as most of the previous studies of Autism focused on boys only.

However, this can change by raising awareness about Autism in both genders and educating people on the truth and about how girls present differently, so that next time, they know what to look for and can actually find, help, and support girls with Autism instead of brushing them aside.

How does an Autistic brain work?

In Autistic people, there are more variations in the brain. So let’s just do a quick biology crash course: There are four lobes—frontal, parietal, occipital, and temporal—within each half. Structures that control everything from movement to thought are located inside these lobes. The cerebral cortex, often known as grey matter, is located above the lobes. Information processing takes place in this area. The cerebral cortex's surface area is increased by the folds in the brain. More information can be processed the larger the surface area, or grey matter.

Going into the more technical side of things, grey matter can form peaks and troughs, which are called gyri and sulci. These creases and wrinkles can take diverse forms in Autistic people, according to San Diego State University researchers. Particularly, there is much greater folding in the right frontal and temporal areas in Autistic brains, as well as in the left parietal and temporal lobes.

Because there are differences in the cerebral Cortex/the grey matter, in the brains of Autistic people, the way they process information, view the world, and interact with it will be different too.

What is the Cerebral Cortex responsible for?

The Cerebral Cortex is responsible for:

  • Determining intelligence and personality
  • Motor function
  • Planning and organisation
  • Touch sensation
  • Processing sensory information
  • Language processing
  • Memory
  • Reasoning
  • Decision making
  • Emotion

Debunking the “dumb” myth

The myth that Autistic people are unable to learn is just a plain lie. Unfortunately, some people still believe that having Autism prevents their ability to learn, grow, and discover new talents and skills. But just because they learn differently or experience things differently, doesn’t mean that they can’t learn anything. If we all learned and experienced things the same way, then there wouldn’t be anything new to learn at all.

Please learn from this or keep in mind that everyone's ability to learn is unique and that autism is a spectrum, it's not set to specific abilities. Because Autistic people process and learn differently, to reach the same degree of learning as their peers, people with Autism may need additional specific support needs, although some people might not. Some could be even easier to teach than allistic people. Autistic individuals who struggle with learning can also benefit from and advance through effective and qualified supporting courses, set at their own pace. Below are some strengths that Autistic people usually have:

Strengths:
  • Attention to detail
  • Visual perception
  • Creative and artistic talents and abilities
  • Mathematical and technical skills
  • Deep knowledge in areas of specific interest
  • Loyal and honest
  • Dedication
  • Strong work ethic
  • Visual sensitivity (autistic people can see up to three times farther than non-autistic people)
  • Pattern recognition
  • Lateral thinking
  • Very observational
  • Deeply focused
  • Good at absorbing and retaining information

Did you know?

There are many successful people with Autism. Famous American film maker and animator Tim Burton, who is known for his popular gothic fantasy and horror films, some of which include Beetlejuice, Edward Scissor Hands, and The Nightmare Before Christmas, is Autistic (he announced it as Asperger's syndrome).

Other famous people with Autism include:

  • Daryl Hannah
  • Satoshi Tajiri
  • Susan Boyle
  • Elon Musk
